First principles

Six tenets, before a single pixel

StepBack exists because setting up a workout must never feel like a chore that makes the user skip the workout (PRD §0.3). Every screen below is judged against these six tenets; when a layout choice is ambiguous, the tenet with the lower number wins.

1 · Simple always works — and simple is not plain

The whole app is three tabs, one builder flow, one stage. Every decision is judged by one question: does this reduce the friction between "I should work out" and "I am working out"? The bar is award-winning UX built from very few concepts — no dashboards, no programs, no upsells.

PRD §0.3 product philosophy; PRD §2 non-goals
2 · The hands-free promise is sacred

Press play, lean the iPad against the wall, step back, follow. Nothing in the player may ever require walking to the device: every step is time-based, rep counts are guidance labels only, and advancement is always automatic. Pause/skip exist for rare intervention, at oversized arm's-length targets.

PRD §0.4, §6.1 (Seven model — time-driven, never rep-driven)
3 · One hero number per screen

The countdown on the stage, total duration on routine detail, active minutes on completion. Everything else steps down through weight and color, not size — the hero-stat role (rounded semibold tabular) exists so screens never shout twice.

DESIGN.md §5 "One hero number per screen"; typography roles table
4 · Across-the-room legibility is a spec, not a vibe

Everything needed to follow the routine must be unmistakable at 3–4 m on an 11″ iPad in landscape: countdown ≥ 25% of stage height, stage text contrast ≥ 7:1 on StageCanvas, work vs rest encoded by hue and layout — never hue alone. Verified in a lit and a dim room before the player is called done.

DESIGN.md `across_the_room` ruleset; PRD §9 acceptance #4
5 · Sound is the primary channel during play

The user is across the room and possibly mid-burpee: voice announces each workout and rest, 3-2-1 beeps precede every transition. No haptics on the stage — the device isn't in hand. The screen confirms what the ears already know.

PRD §6.4 audio design; DESIGN.md §7 "Sound is the primary feedback channel"
6 · Every state is designed, including the honest ones

Empty states teach the next action; a partial session records honestly and gets a smaller acknowledgment, never a guilt trip; a missing catalog id still renders via the name snapshot; a broken streak simply shows the new number. No dead spinners, no raw errors, no shame states.

PRD §5.7, §7 (honest by construction), §4.1 (id resilience)

Foundation · Appearance

The stage is always dark

Browsing surfaces are native OS 26 — semantic colors that adapt to light and dark automatically, zero colorScheme branches. The player is different: the moment Play is tapped the app goes full-screen onto StageCanvas #0B0F14, identical at noon and midnight. Same convention as video players: a wall-propped iPad must not glow like a lamp in a dim room or wash out in a bright one.

The rules

  • Work vs rest is encoded twice — segment hue (StageWork vs StageRest) and layout (work leads with the countdown; rest leads with the next workout name). Never hue alone (DESIGN.md `across_the_room.state_identity`) — the state must survive color-blindness and glare.
  • On the stage, work and rest use appearance-invariant accents — StageWork #46A2FF / StageRest #3BD6C3 — because the canvas is permanently dark. Browsing surfaces continue using the adaptive Pulse Azure and Recover Mint pairs.
  • Hues are accents, never floods: the segment hue lives in the kicker, progress bar, and pause button — the canvas stays #0B0F14 and the countdown stays StageText #F5F7FA (contrast ≈ 17:1, comfortably past the ≥ 7:1 rule).
  • Get-ready wears mint, not azure: it is a breathing segment ("step back now"), and mint's meaning is exactly that.
Screen group 1

Onboarding — one screen, then inside

Onboarding does exactly two things: say what the app is, and get the user inside fast (PRD §5.1). One welcome screen, one button. On first launch the app seeds three starter routines — short, medium, long — so the Routines home is never an empty dead end and the first play-through can happen within seconds.

Why it's right

  • No feature tour, no permission pre-prompts. The three rows are the app's entire mental model; anything more would violate tenet 1. Copy is calm and second-person (DESIGN.md §8).
  • Seeded starters do the real onboarding. Bootstrap seeds Quick Core (short), Everyday Reset (medium), and Full-Body Forty (long) before this cover is dismissed, built only from catalog workouts and defined as data alongside the catalog. They are ordinary routines after seeding: editable, deletable, no special casing (PRD §5.1).
  • Seeding is idempotent across devices: seed only when the store has no routines at all, so CloudKit-synced content wins over re-seeding. Onboarding-seen state is @AppStorage, never CloudKit.
  • Tokens: icon tile rows use PulseAzureSoft fills with PulseAzure glyphs; the CTA is the app's one accent. No red, no gradients beyond the app icon artwork itself.

Build it with

  • Full-screen cover shown once on @AppStorage("welcomeSeen"); bootstrap owns idempotent seeding, while Get Started only records welcome-seen and dismisses.
  • All strings from the String Catalog, including the privacy footnote (PRD §3.1).
Tab 1

Routines home — play is one tap from launch

The home answers three glanceable questions — what do I have, when did I last do it, how consistent am I — and puts Play directly on every card (PRD §5.2). Once the first session exists, a compact motivation strip sits above the grid: day streak and this week's active minutes. Before that, the strip is omitted rather than showing zeroes. Two numbers, one line, no charts, no rings.

Card anatomy (DESIGN.md §4 "Routine card")

  • Name (body medium) → hero duration (hero-stat role, tabular) → workout count + category-mix dots (non-interactive hue dots, one per distinct category, catalog order) → stats footnote "Last done 2 days ago · 14×" (relative date via Foundation, count via plural-aware format string).
  • Never-played routines stay honest: the stats footnote reads "Not played yet" until the first session exists.
  • The inline Play button is the one-tap-from-launch promise (PRD §5.2, acceptance #1): card tap navigates to detail, Play tap goes straight to the pre-roll. Two distinct targets, both ≥ 44 pt.
  • Delete never rides a card gesture. It lives in routine detail and context menus, with the system destructive role and confirmation (DESIGN.md §5) — the only red in the app.
  • Empty state (only possible if the user deletes the starters): ContentUnavailableView-shaped — icon tile, "No routines yet", one line of guidance, primary action New Routine, secondary Restore starter routines (PRD §5.2). Restore inserts fresh copies of only the missing starter identifiers, so repeated restores are safe and existing starters are untouched.

Motivation strip rules

  • One compact row, two quiet stat pairs — not a card, not a chart, no rings (DESIGN.md §4). Streak counts days with ≥ 1 completed session, computed from local calendar days at read time (PRD §7).
  • The strip is hidden until the first-ever session row exists; the initial routine grid needs no dead chrome or zero-value shame state.
  • A broken streak simply shows the new number — no shame states, no "you lost it" copy.
Tab 1 · Plans

Weekly plans — Today first, My Week always visible

A plan is seven weekday buckets, each holding zero or more routines; empty days are Rest. The selected My Week schedule leads Routines with one Today card, while one quiet row below the routine list is the only management entry. There are no cursors, week numbers, Skip, Start/Stop, or completion lifecycle.

Interaction and state rules

  • One My Week selection. The first saved plan is selected automatically. Other overviews offer Set as My Week; deleting the current plan leaves a chooser instead of promoting another plan.
  • Done is read-time truth: each slot matches one completed session of that routine on that local day. Ad-hoc runs count, duplicate slots require duplicate sessions, and abandoned runs never count.
  • Overview and picker: overview has seven day rows with today accented, Rest visible, and removed-routine snapshots preserved. The plain picker appears for multiple plans with quiet “5 days · Push, Pull, Legs…” summaries.
  • VoiceOver: Today is one descriptive group with Play as its action; the strip is one summary element, never seven dots. Overview rows combine weekday, routines, duration, and done state.
Tab 1 · Push

Routine detail — the list reads exactly as it plays

Total duration is the screen's one hero number. Below it, the step list is a faithful preview of the compiled timeline (PRD §6.2): work rows with their timing summaries, and every rest rendered between rows as its own quiet Recover-Mint row. What you read here is literally what the player will run.

Design decisions

  • One source of truth for the total: the hero number is the compiled timeline's sum — the same integer-seconds figure shown on the card, in the builder's live total, and as the player's overall progress denominator (PRD §6.2). If they ever disagree, that's a bug, not a rounding choice.
  • Rest rows are RecoverMintSoft with primary text and a RecoverMint icon — mint remains the rest identity without carrying small readable text. They render between steps because that's where they happen; trailing rest is omitted (a routine never ends on a rest).
  • Duplicate is a first-class secondary action because copy-then-tweak is the natural way to iterate on a routine (PRD §5.2). Edit opens the builder pre-populated; Delete confirms and preserves session history via name snapshots (PRD §4.4).
  • Agent provenance is deliberately quiet: an agent-created or agent-edited routine adds one footnote in the summary group. It is context, not an alert or activity feed.
  • Rep guidance renders inline in the summary ("30 s · ~20 reps") in the footnote role — guidance only, never a timing input (PRD §6.1).
  • WorkoutVisual small tiles (1:1, category-hue soft fill ≈ hue at 13% over surface, hue glyph) — the same media-ready component as gallery and stage; photos drop into these exact slots later without reflow (PRD §4.7).

Accessibility

  • Each step row is one VoiceOver element: "Bridge, 30 seconds, 3 sets, 10 seconds between sets." Rest rows read "Rest, 15 seconds."
  • At accessibility Dynamic Type sizes the timing summary wraps below the name; the hero stat scales with its text style and stays the only large number.
Tab 2

Gallery — a library, not a queue

The full workout catalog (≥ 80 built-ins across eight categories) plus the user's own additions, organized by category and searchable. Browsing never mutates a routine until the user explicitly adds (PRD §5.3). Every tile is the media-ready WorkoutVisual slot: today a monogram, later a photo or video loop — same slot, zero reflow.

The media-ready contract, annotated

  • Every tile above is the WorkoutVisual slot (PRD §4.7): 1:1 aspect, concentric radius, category-hue soft fill (hue at ~13% over surface — the same recipe as PulseAzureSoft) with the category SF Symbol as the placeholder glyph. When media ships, photos/video loops resolve via mediaKey into these exact slots — same size, same radius, zero reflow. Feature code never asks "is there media?"; it asks the component to render the workout.
  • Monogram = category identity, deliberately: until per-workout media exists, a per-workout illustration would be fake. The hue + symbol pair is honest, scannable, and identical everywhere the workout appears (gallery, builder row, detail, stage).
  • Search covers built-ins and custom workouts by localized name; results keep category grouping. Empty search result: ContentUnavailableView.search.
  • Custom workouts are marked "Yours" in the caption slot, never with a different card shape or color. Deleting one that routines reference is allowed — steps keep working via the name snapshot (PRD §4.2).
  • On iPhone the same grid runs 3-up; category headers become navigation into full category pages in compact width.
Tab 2 · Push

Workout detail — lightweight, one screen

Media slot, name, category, focus-area chips, and one action: Add to Routine. For custom workouts this screen also carries notes and Edit/Delete (PRD §5.3).

Decisions

  • Add to Routine opens a small sheet: choose an existing routine or New Routine (which opens the builder with this workout as step 1). Browsing never mutates a routine until this explicit add — the gallery is a library (PRD §5.3).
  • 4:3 header ratio comes from the DESIGN.md `media_readiness.slots` table (workout_detail: "4:3"); gallery cards are 1:1, builder rows 1:1 small, stage region 4:3. These four ratios are the whole media contract.
  • Focus-area chips come from catalog focusAreas ids with localized names — display-only in v1 (future filtering, PRD §4.1).
  • Custom workout variant: same layout plus a notes paragraph and Edit/Delete in the toolbar; Delete uses the destructive role and explains that routines keep the name snapshot.
  • Instructions text is deferred (instructionsKey reserved, PRD §10) — no empty "How to" section ships; the screen is complete without it.
The make-or-break screen

Routine builder — composing feels like arranging index cards

The "never a chore" promise lives or dies here (PRD §0.3, §5.4). The step list reads exactly like the routine will play — rest rows between steps — with drag handles, inline per-step controls in 5 s increments, and a floating glass bar holding Add Workouts and the live computed total. The mockup shows the §5.4 sample routine mid-edit, with the Bridge step expanded.

Why this builder wins the "never a chore" bet

  • Multi-select in one pass: the §5.4 sample — five workouts — lands in the list with one sheet visit. New steps arrive with smart defaults (30 s work, 1 set, no set-rest, 15 s rest-after), and the previous step's values become the defaults for the next add within a session, because routines are rhythmic (PRD §5.4).
  • Steppers and wheels, never keyboards: all durations move in 5 s increments; sets in 1s; rep guidance in 5s with an Off position. Free-text seconds would be friction and a localization hazard.
  • The live total is always visible in the floating bar and updates with every change — the user calibrates a "40-minute routine" while composing. It is the same compiled figure shown everywhere else (PRD §6.2).
  • Reorder with standard drag handles; rest-after values travel with their step, so the mint rows follow their owners (DESIGN.md §4 "Rest row").
  • Save validates one thing only: ≥ 1 step and a non-empty name (a default name like "Routine 3" is suggested). There is no way to build an invalid routine — everything else has defaults.
  • Timing summary grammar is a format string: "30 s × 3 · 10 s between sets", with "~20 reps" appended when guidance is set. Tabular numerals keep the column aligned.
  • In regular width the builder pairs the step list with the picker side-by-side instead of a covering sheet (DESIGN.md §5); the iPhone gets the sheet shown here.

Compact stage & completion

Across-the-room rules (all cited from DESIGN.md `across_the_room`)

  • Exactly one hero: the countdown, ≥ 25% of stage height on iPad landscape, stage-numerals role (the app's single fixed-size exception). Nothing else on the stage competes in size — ever.
  • Minimums: workout name ≥ title scale, set indicator and next-up ≥ headline scale, progress bar full-width and ≥ 6 pt tall, all stage text ≥ 7:1 contrast on StageCanvas (including StageTextDim at 80% opacity).
  • State identity is doubled: hue (azure/mint) and layout (countdown-led vs next-up-led). A color-blind user in a glary room still knows whether to move or breathe.
  • Verification is part of done: 3 m test, lit room and dim room, both segment types, iPad landscape + portrait and iPhone (PRD acceptance #4).

Behavior contract (PRD §5.5–§6.4)

  • Pre-roll: 5 s "Get ready" (configurable) in the mint composition, first workout announced by voice — time to step back.
  • Controls: pause/resume (largest, segment hue), skip, back (restart segment; twice = previous), End (confirms). All ≥ 64 pt targets on the StageSurface glass bar — the second of the two permitted custom glass elements. Mac: space / arrows / ⎋.
  • Audio: name announced at each work start (with set position when sets > 1), "Rest" + next name at rest starts, 3-2-1 beeps before every transition, distinct work-start tone. Ducks politely under the user's music; never stops it (PRD §6.4). Voice and tones independently toggleable.
  • Never sleeps, never loses progress: idle timer disabled while playing; backgrounding auto-pauses and resumes at the same segment with a fresh 3-2-1 (PRD §6.3). Rotation is free; the layout adapts, never locks.
  • No haptics on transitions — the device is across the room; sound is the channel (DESIGN.md `haptics`).
  • Elapsed/remaining are integer seconds from the compiled timeline — the same source of truth as every displayed total (PRD §6.2); the progress denominator is the routine's 5 min 30 s.
Where the design effort went: everything on the stage exists to be read at 3–4 m or heard. If a proposed addition (calories, heart rate, a motivational quote) can't justify itself at that distance, it doesn't go on the stage — it goes nowhere, because the PRD explicitly cut it.
Tab 3

Settings — minimal, honest

The core player controls, two lines of truth, and one Mac-only agent-access section. No account, no sign-in, no premium (PRD §5.6).

Decisions

  • Toggles tint PulseAzure — the app accent applies to switches, keeping the one-accent rule intact.
  • Get-ready row pushes a wheel picker in 5 s increments (0–30 s); the mint icon tile is honest: get-ready is a breathing segment.
  • Sync status is a read-only account-status line, not a control: "Checking…", "Up to date", or "iCloud unavailable — changes stay on this device" (designed degraded state, PRD §5.7). SwiftData does not expose a truthful per-sync event or last-sync timestamp, so the row never invents one. Never an error dialog.
  • Agent Access is Mac-only ordinary Form content: the toggle gates the inbox immediately, the reveal button opens the app-owned bridge folder, and the footer states the non-destructive boundary. It adds no custom glass and never exposes a database path.
  • No "rate the app", no links farm, no analytics toggle (there is nothing to toggle — zero analytics, PRD §8).
